Case study · 14-day audit window

Faster lead response and follow-up recovery in 14 days

How a regional clinic operator tightened intake, cleared stale pipeline stages, and sized a modeled revenue range—without adding another tool on day one.

Illustrative composite. This narrative blends patterns from real operational audits; it is not a named client, not a guarantee, and uses no real patient or customer data. Metrics below are ranges and methodology examples from CRM exports and team interviews unless labeled hypothetical. Your results will vary with baseline, staffing, and seasonality.

Context

A three-location dental and aesthetic clinic group (anonymized; ~45 staff) relied on web forms, WhatsApp, and referral partners for new inquiries. Front desk and treatment coordinators shared one CRM, but ownership after the first reply was unclear.

Stated pain: slow first responses on high-intent channels, and follow-ups that stopped after one attempt—leads sat in “contacted” with no next step while coordinators focused on chair time.

14-day audit scope

We measured what their systems already recorded—no invented industry benchmarks.

  • Lead timestamps — inquiry created → first human reply (median and 90th percentile by channel)
  • Pipeline hygiene — count and age of leads in “new,” “contacted,” and “stale” (no activity 14+ days)
  • Handoff map — who owns intake vs. scheduling vs. treatment plan follow-up
  • Volume baseline — qualified inquiries per week (forms + messenger, excluding spam)
  • Conversion snapshot — booked consultation ÷ qualified inquiries (trailing 30 days, as reported in CRM)

Findings

  • Intake: after-hours inquiries waited until the next business day with no queue or auto-acknowledgment; partners could not see status.
  • SLA: no documented first-response target; median first reply during business hours was roughly 4–7 hours (CRM-derived range for the audit window).
  • Handoffs: coordinators assumed front desk had replied; duplicate or zero touches on the same lead.
  • CRM hygiene: ~50–70 leads aged 14+ days in “contacted” with no scheduled task; stages did not distinguish “waiting on patient” vs. “we owe a follow-up.”

Interventions (process, not “AI”)

  • Routing rules — channel owner for first touch; escalation if unclaimed after 30 minutes in business hours.
  • Business-hours SLA — target first human reply within 90 minutes for qualified inquiries (published internally).
  • Follow-up cadence — three-touch template (day 0, 2, 7) with explicit “close or re-queue” on day 10.
  • Weekly stale review — named owner clears or re-engages aged pipeline; stage definitions updated in CRM.
  • Partner visibility — lightweight status note for referral sources (no new software in the pilot).

Outcomes (careful language)

Figures below mix client-reported observations during the 14-day pilot window with an illustrative scenario for sizing. They are not audited financial statements.

  • Lead response (methodology) Median first-response during business hours moved from a CRM-derived ~4–7 h baseline toward a ~45–90 min range while the SLA and routing rules were active (client-reported; channel mix dependent).
  • Follow-up recovery In the illustrative scenario, ~55 stale leads were re-queued; ~12–18 booked a consultation after the three-touch cadence (client-reported range). Remaining stale records were closed-lost with reason codes.
  • Pipeline stages “Contacted” without next step dropped from ~50–70 to under 15 open items at week two (client-reported).

Modeled revenue impact (hypothetical inputs)

If baseline inquiry volume and conversion hold, a modest improvement in booked consultations can be sized without claiming a fixed dollar outcome.

Assumptions (illustrative)
~85 qualified inquiries / month; consultation booking rate 22% baseline; illustrative improvement to 25–26% (+3–4 pp); average accepted treatment plan €1,100–€1,300 (hypothetical, regional private-pay band).
Formula
additional revenue / month ≈ inquiries × (conversionafter − conversionbefore) × avg case value
Modeled estimate (range)
85 × 0.03 × €1,200 ≈ €3,060 to 85 × 0.04 × €1,300 ≈ €4,420 per month—labeled illustrative, not guaranteed.

We present this as a sensitivity band, not proof of ROI. Seasonality, case mix, and discounting can move realized revenue outside the range.
